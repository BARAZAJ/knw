Our Mission

"To protect, preserve and conserve the water resources of Lake Kyoga and River Nile for the benefit of current and future generations through community engagement, education, and sustainable environmental practices."

Sustainability
Community
Protection
Education
01

Protect & Preserve

Safeguarding the natural ecosystem of Lake Kyoga and River Nile through sustainable environmental protection and active community engagement across Uganda.

Key Activities:

  • Water quality monitoring and testing
  • Wetland restoration and protection
  • Pollution prevention and cleanup
  • Habitat conservation for aquatic species
  • Riverbank stabilization projects

Our Impact:

50+ Communities Protected
200+ Species Monitored
15 Restoration Sites
02

Raise Awareness

Promoting understanding of the importance of River Nile and Lake Kyoga by educating local communities about environmental protection and sustainable practices.

Key Activities:

  • Community education workshops
  • School environmental programs
  • Public awareness campaigns
  • Training local water guardians
  • Media outreach and communications

Our Impact:

1,200+ People Trained
30+ Schools Reached
85+ Workshops Conducted
03

Build Partnerships

Collaborating with government agencies and organizations to develop strong alliances, strengthening our conservation efforts through strategic partnerships.

Key Activities:

  • Government agency collaboration
  • International organization partnerships
  • Community group alliances
  • Academic research partnerships
  • Corporate sustainability initiatives

Our Impact:

25+ Active Partners
5 Government Agencies
10+ International Links

Our Vision

"Clean, healthy waters of Lake Kyoga and River Nile that sustain thriving ecosystems and prosperous communities for generations to come."

2025 Goals

  • Establish 10 new community monitoring stations
  • Train 500 additional water guardians
  • Complete 5 major restoration projects

2030 Vision

  • 100% community participation in conservation
  • Zero pollution incidents in protected areas
  • Self-sustaining ecosystem health
